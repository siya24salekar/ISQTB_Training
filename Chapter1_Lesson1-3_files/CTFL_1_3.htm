The role of software testing

Software testing’s role can be defined as reducing the risk of problems when using the software, and also to contribute to the quality of the software / system.

By performing well-planned and valid tests, the level of risk can be significantly reduced of an issue occurring with the software. If any defects can be found before a commercial software product is released, then this can be a big plus from a business perspective. No business wants their customers to find problems with their products. Imagine, if a word processing application did not correctly save a document. This would result in unhappy customers, falling sales and damage to business reputation. As you can see, a defects impact can ‘snowball’ into something much larger. So, if software testing can detect such defects before a products release, then we can consider the risk of potential problems to be reduced.     

Some types of software may require specific testing in order to meet certain types of requirements, for example legal and contractual requirements, or industry standards.

 

Legal and Contractual Requirements

If a software development is being developed, the entity who has requested the development may have a certain set of criteria that the software must meet. For example, the software may need to meet the requirement of being able to process a specific amount of transactions per hour. Or, the software may have to meet certain security requirements. For example, software related to a medical practice may be legally required to hide sensitive information (e.g. people names / addresses etc.).

 

Industry Standard Requirements 

Today, software is used in a vast amount of (if not all) industries. In many cases, certain industry standards must be adhered to. For example, a factory producing Ethernet switches or routers will have to ensure that the appropriate Ethernet standards and protocols (e.g. IEEE 802.3) are followed to ensure the correct behavior of the device. Often software can directly or indirectly impact human life. For example, software designed to be used in an Air Traffic Control system would have to meet stringent industry standards. This is because a defect could result in a catastrophic loss of life scenario.
