Lesson 5-1

Test Organization and Independence

We know that by performing testing and using reviews we can detect defects. However, there is another factor that can come into play that will improve the effectiveness of testing and reviews, and this called 'independence'. The term independence, does not mean that the person doing the testing should work alone, but is defined as an approach that follows the rule that the more independent the testing is; the greater the effectiveness.

The thinking behind this theory is that someone who is close to the project, for example a developer, lacks objectivity. It is not in the best interest of the developer to criticize his own work and is therefore unethical. Also, they would have probably exhausted their own knowledge of how to test the code during unit tests earlier on in the project. Its an old concept, but "two eyes are better than one" actually still holds firm in today's software development environment. For example, if you asked several different people who each work in different departments to test the software, they would all have different results. This is because their knowledge guides them to different areas of the software and how it is used. So by using different people we are increasing the amount and also the type of defects found. Careful consideration should be used when selecting who is to perform the testing, as it will reflect significantly in the results.

Often, complex or safety critical projects, multiple levels of testing, with some or all of the levels performed by independent testers. Developers can still participate in the testing, especially in the lower levels, but care should be taken in respect to their objectivity. Outside of developing and testing, other roles or job functions can be used to perform independent testing and can each bring associated benefits if chosen correctly. For example; project managers, quality managers, business and domain experts, infrastructure, support or IT operations personnel.

There are several options in a typical software development environment for testing independence:

  • No independent testers used (developers testing their own software code)

  • Independent testers within the development teams

  • Independent test team or group within the organization (reporting to project management or executive management)

  • Independent testers from the business organization or user community

  • Independent test specialists for specific test types such as usability testers, security testers or certification testers (who certify a software product against standards and regulations)

  • Independent testers outsourced or external to the organization

The following list displays the benefits of testing independence:

  • Independent testers are unbiased and detect a variety of defects

  • Independent testers can verify assumptions people made during specification and implementation of the system

While the benefits of testing independence can be clearly seen, there are however some negative aspects to it, which if not observed and monitored correctly can cause significant impact to the effectiveness of the testing and even affect the moral of the testers involved


Potential drawbacks of testing independence:

  • Isolation from the developers (if working completely independently)

  • Developers can lose responsibility for quality

  • Independent testers are sometimes viewed as bottlenecks, and blamed for project delays
