Risk and Testing

In relation to software developments, we can consider risk to be the chance of an event, hazard, threat or situation occurring which results in undesirable consequences or a potential problem. The level of risk is typically determined by the 'likelihood' of the event happening combined with the 'impact of the event. Risk is normally categorized into one of the following two areas:

Project Risks

A project risk is defined as any risks that are related the project’s capability to deliver on it's objectives. The following list represents potential project related risks:

  • Personnel issues

  • Political issues, such as:

  • Problems with testers communicating their needs and test results

  • Failure by the team to follow up on information found in testing and reviews

  • Organizational factors:

  • Skill, training and staff shortages

  • Improper attitude toward or expectations of testing (e.g., not appreciating the value of finding defects during testing)

  • Technical issues:
    Problems in defining the right requirements
    The extent to which requirements cannot be met given existing constraints
    Test environment not ready on time
    Late data conversion, migration planning and development and testing data conversion/migration tools
    Low quality of the design, code, configuration data, test data and tests

  • Supplier issues:
    Failure of a third party
    Contractual issues

If a risk is present, then the following three tasks must be carried out by the test manager:

Analyzing
Managing
Mitigating

Ideally the test manager should be following a well established process or set of guidelines. Information relating to risks can be found in the ‘Standard for Software Test Documentation’ (IEEE Std 829-1998), under the section 'outline for test plans'. This information states that risks and contingencies to be stated within the test plan.

 

Product Risks

A product risk can be considered to be a potential failure area within the software or system.

Some examples of product risks are:

  • The potential that the software/hardware could cause harm to an individual or company

  • Poor software characteristics (e.g., functionality, reliability, usability and performance)

  • Failure-prone software delivered

  • Poor data integrity and quality (e.g., data migration issues, data conversion problems, data transport problems, violation of data standards)

  • Software that does not perform its intended functions

So where do we start testing when considering risks?....By analyzing each product risk we can decide if testing should be used to reduce the risk of an adverse effect occurring, or perhaps to reduce the impact of an adverse effect when it occurs.

Make sure you understand the following keywords associated with this lesson: 

Product risk:

A risk directly related to the test object.

Project risk:

A risk related to management and control of the (test) project, e.g. lack of staffing, strict deadlines, changing requirements, etc.

Risk:

A factor that could result in future negative consequences; usually expressed as impact and likelihood.

Risk-based testing:

An approach to testing to reduce the level of product risks and inform stakeholders of their status, starting in the initial stages of a project. It involves the identification of product risks and the use of risk levels to guide the test process
